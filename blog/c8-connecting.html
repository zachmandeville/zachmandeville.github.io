Have you tried any of the trust-building, sincerity excercises? How did it go?

Oh, I'm all ABOUT sincerity! Sincerely! I've done a fair amount of comedy in my life working, with different groups within the city. Before that, I had creative groups around zines or music too. In each, sincerity was required to build trust and in that trust came support. This is true with the group--being sincere in your art, and wanting others to do well, helps you receive and give feedback. This translates into stronger art, and a stronger network of people to help with each other's art. This dynamic was present between the performer and audience too. If the audience feels you are just making things up, and not believing in what it is you're doing, then they will check out. Actual sincerity is how you build trust with that audience, who wants to support you through a good set. When that trust is built between auidence and performers, then the show is absolutely magical.

I have not done the exact trust exercises he mentions, but have done variations of them in these groups.

Delightful! The quality I'm enjoying in each of these exercises is how physical they are--or like how there is body sensations coming from the mental journeys. The Political Awareness Exercise

Think of a difficult situation from your present or past, when there was some conflict or disagreement, something real, something that has some meaning and potency for you.

The one that popped into my mind initially was a disagreement I had on my second-to-last day of work. I was the product manager of this Analytics software, and the team that created and maintained it had left the company with myself leaving shortly after. So I was working to transition responsibilities to a new development team. One of these responsibilities was with terminology throughout the software. Sometimes our definitions change, or we find that the definition we used was not clear to our customer. To fix them, you would go into the code, find the field in which the definition was listed, and change it.

I wanted this job to be able to be done by our upper-level support people. The new product manager refused this, though, saying that only developers should touch the code and support could move through proper developer channels if they wanted to propose a wording change.

Describe the situation as if you were 100% reasonable

Having worked with the software for a year, and having worked with the support team far longer, I know what areas they are experts in and what things they are capable of. I also had this belief that, working in a tech company, our support should understand the tech they're working with, and so any time there's a chance to expand their knowledge, we should take it as it's good for everyone. I also knew that the development team taking over did not understand the end-use of the software. They were learning the code, but not how people used it or approached it. So they would be having to turn to Support constantly to figure out what these words meant, or how customers read them. Because of this, it felt inefficient and frankly rude to not integrate support more into the development of the product, to better get that customer perspective at a code level.

Describe the situation as if the other person was 100% reasonable

We are working with technology that affects thousands of people in their day-to-day operations. We can not risk this technology going down because someone didn't understand what they were doing, and changed some code they shouldn't have. Allowing someone that I do not work with directly to be within the codebase provides too strong of a risk. While I am learning this code, the best thing I can rely on is the principles learned in the dev world--including the principle of least permissions. So I will use this as a guide, and provide a known channel for support to communicate with us without extending breakable code to them. This is also an area where I do not fully know the code and product yet (as I'm transitioning to it) so I want the borders and processes to be as bold and narrow as possible as I gain my footing.

Any other musings?

I have reservations against the mainstream tech world (or Silicon Valley). Reservations isnt' the right word--I have deep resentment against them. I can be empathetic to people within that culture, but I worry about any empathy of that culture as a whole. The fact that empathy has to be taught to them in a book, as a way to make meetings stronger and your team succeed more, doesn't really boost my feelings about that culture at all. I love mindfulness, but not when it's being given like some disgusting vegetable hidden within the cheese and potatoes of a meal. It makes me wonder why this has to be translated so drastically for this crowd.
